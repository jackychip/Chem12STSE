Nanotechnology in Superhydrophobic Coating

By: Jacky Zhang

Last edited: September 27th, 2024

ABSTRACT

Nanotechnology is the manipulation of atoms and molecules at the nanoscale to develop, manufacture, and apply systems with optimized material properties. This technology is especially effective in coatings, where nanoparticles strengthen durability, water resistance, and self-cleaning properties. Superhydrophobic surfaces, inspired by the lotus leaf, repel water through Van der Waals forces, hydrogen bonding, and covalent bonding, resulting in long-lasting and functional coatings. These surfaces offer significant benefits in medical, industrial, and environmental applications by reducing pathogen accumulation, maintenance costs, and chemical use. However, challenges such as high costs, scalability, and environmental degradation remain. Improvements in hierarchical structures, self-healing materials, and inexpensive alternatives promise to improve the effectiveness and versatility of superhydrophobic coatings, making them a promising field for future research and development.

PAPER

    Nanotechnology involves the design, manufacture, and application of manipulated atoms and molecules at the nanoscale. To put this into perspective, if the diameter of a marble were one nanometer, the Earth’s diameter would be roughly one meter. At this scale, particle size significantly impacts material characteristics due to the dominance of electrostatic forces and quantum effects over gravitational forces, resulting in optimized material properties [2][12]. This paper will explore the use and effectiveness of these optimizations in superhydrophobic coatings, their global impact, and potential improvements.

    Superhydrophobic coatings are thin surface layers designed to repel water. A surface is classified as superhydrophobic if it has a high water contact angle (angle formed between a liquid droplet and a solid surface) greater than 150°, low contact angle hysteresis (the maximum angle as the droplet moves forward) less than 10°, and a low sliding angle (the tilt angle at which a droplet starts to slide off a surface) less than 5° [4, 9]. The optimization that nanotechnology provides becomes very apparent in superhydrophobic coatings. This involves applying nanoparticles to surfaces to enhance their properties. The result offers benefits such as increased durability, resistance to water and dirt, and self-cleaning capabilities, mimicking the properties of a lotus leaf (Figure 1). At the nanoscale, Van der Waals forces, which result from the interaction of dipoles in molecules, support the initial adhesion of nanoparticles to the surface. These forces are significant because nanoparticles possess a high surface area-to-volume ratio. Additionally, hydrogen bonding, which occurs when a hydrogen atom is attracted to an electronegative atom, can form on the surface, improving the coating’s stability and adhesion. In some advanced superhydrophobic coatings, nanoparticles form covalent bonds with the surface, which are significantly stronger than Van der Waals forces and hydrogen bonds, resulting in a more durable and long-lasting coating. For instance, silicon dioxide (SiO₂) coatings, which have self-cleaning and anti-corrosive properties, form covalent bonds with oxygen atoms on the surface, creating a strong and stable layer. The chemical properties of nanoparticles allow them to self-assemble into organized structures, with Van der Waals forces, hydrogen bonding, and occasionally covalent bonding all contributing to this process. This results in a uniform and highly functional coating with properties such as hydrophobicity and anti-corrosion [1, 3, 10].

    Superhydrophobic surfaces offer significant benefits in various settings. In medical environments, they can reduce the accumulation of bacteria, viruses, and other pathogens, which is crucial for maintaining cleanliness. In industries where workers are exposed to wet conditions, such as construction or firefighting, these coatings can keep clothing and equipment dry, improving comfort, safety, mobility, and efficiency [6]. Additionally, superhydrophobic coatings can lower maintenance costs due to their self-cleaning properties. Water droplets roll off, carrying dirt and debris, which helps keep buildings cleaner for longer periods. Applying these coatings can also protect materials from corrosion and wear. For example, in the automotive industry, coating car exteriors can prevent rust and extend the vehicle’s lifespan, leading to cost savings and reduced resource consumption over time. In agriculture, superhydrophobic coatings on equipment can reduce water usage for cleaning purposes. Traditional cleaning methods often involve harsh chemicals that can harm the environment, but superhydrophobic surfaces reduce the reliance on these chemicals, resulting in less environmental pollution [1, 7]. However, there are challenges associated with developing and applying superhydrophobic coatings. The high cost of raw materials, equipment, and labour can limit the adoption of this technology. The precision required in the application process and the need for consistent quality control make it difficult to produce these coatings in large quantities. Additionally, environmental factors such as UV radiation, abrasion, and chemical exposure can degrade the effectiveness of superhydrophobic coatings over time. This necessitates reapplication, which involves additional resource consumption and potential environmental impact from the disposal of old coatings [1].

    To further develop this technology, it is essential to consider some measures that will assist in its evolution in terms of durability, functionality, and affordability. The use of a multilayered hierarchical structure in coating design can improve resistance to damage of the superhydrophobic layer by providing structural support and protecting it from mechanical deterioration [5]. Such coating, which is comprised of microcapsules containing healing agents that may repair the surface when scratched, extends the life of the coating and reduces the need to maintain the surface (Figure 2) [13]. To make superhydrophobic surfaces more cost-effective, researchers are exploring methods like chemical etching and using waste materials such as agricultural residues and industrial byproducts. Additionally, combining passivated ZnO with Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) offers a low-cost solution for corrosion protection [11]. The modification of the coatings through the formulation of protective shields that can endure UV rays, abrasion, and chemicals is critical for the development of the durability of this technology. Given this, this technology has the potential to become increasingly powerful, cost-effective, and versatile in its applications [1].

    Superhydrophobic surfaces are great for repelling water, self-cleaning, and preventing ice buildup because of their lotus leaf effect. However, their durability can be an issue due to environmental factors. Depending on the application, alternatives like hydrophilic surfaces (which prevent fogging), oleophobic surfaces (which repel oils), icephobic surfaces (which prevent ice formation), and antimicrobial coatings (which prevent pathogen growth) may be more suitable [14].

    Nanotechnology in coatings gives us an exciting look into the future of material science. By manipulating particles at the nanoscale, we can create surfaces with amazing properties that could transform various industries. Although there are challenges, the potential benefits make this a promising area for continued research and development.
